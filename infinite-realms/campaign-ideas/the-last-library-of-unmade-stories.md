The Last Library of Unmade Stories

A Genre-Bending / Mythic-Fantasy campaign — Tone: Wondering & Uneasy — Full Campaign (10–14 sessions) — Medium-Hard

Campaign Overview

Title: The Last Library of Unmade Stories
Campaign Type / Genre: Mythic Fantasy / Narrative-Magic (a book-magic world where stories literally reshape reality)
Estimated Length: Full Campaign — 10–14 sessions (3–4 hours each)
Player Level Range: Start 1 → Finish 11–12 (tiered growth as players "write" themselves into existence)
Tone (keywords): uncanny wonder, bittersweet, ethical paradoxes, playful dread, narrative mystery
Core Premise (2–4 sentences):
A hidden Library sits between the cracks of the world — shelves of unwritten tomes whose unwritten pages shape possibility. When the Library begins to leak unmade stories into the world, fictions become flesh and forgotten possibilities stalk towns. The party is recruited (or pulled) to find the Library’s lost Keys, discover who’s writing history without consent, and decide whether to preserve the Archive, burn it, or become its new Authors.

What “Medium-Hard” Difficulty Means Here

Encounters: Mix of social, investigation, and unusual combats. Expect puzzles that require lateral thinking and meta-answers (solvable via creative player input). Combat can be punishing when players' past choices have altered the narrative fabric.

Puzzles/Survival: Story mechanics (see Unique Mechanics) create resource pressure; failures may rewrite character memories or stats temporarily.

Consequences: Player choices have tangible, world-altering consequences (NPCs remember different histories, towns warp)—not instant TPK, but heavy narrative penalties.

Story Arc (Full Campaign — 10–14 sessions)

Three acts with a mid-campaign twist

Act I — Loose Pages (Sessions 1–4): Strange fictions leak into the world. PCs collect first clues, gain Story Threads, meet a Librarian, and stop localized anomalies.

Act II — Ink and Authority (Sessions 5–9): The party hunts Library Keys, meets rival Authors, and uncovers that the Library is failing — someone is erasing whole genres. Moral complexity rises as the party is offered authorship.

Act III — Authorship (Sessions 10–14): Final choice: restore the Library’s protocols, take authorship (gain world-shaping power with cost), or destroy the Archive (end narrative magic but leave the world irreversibly altered). Endings vary drastically.

Session Breakdown (12-session example)
Session 1 — “A Town with a New Ending”

Focus: Investigation / Social

Key Events: Players arrive at Little Mire where everyone now believes the river runs left instead of right; a fictional nationalist hero walks the streets. Clues: odd paper-scent in the air, typewriter keys in mud. First minor encounter: Errant Paragraphs (small animated paper swarms). Reward: 1 Story Thread (see mechanics).

Session 2 — “The Blot”

Focus: Exploration / Puzzle

Key Events: Find a black blot of ink swallowing a crossroads. Solve a riddle puzzle to free trapped memories (skill challenge: Investigation + History + Performance). Combat: Ink Wyrm (reskinned constrictor with acid ink attacks). Gain Librarian’s Note.

Session 3 — “The Librarian & The Lost Key”

Focus: Social / Roleplay

Key Events: Meet Quill, a half-formed Librarian who’s losing syllables daily. Quill tasks them to recover a stolen Library Key from a traveling troupe of forgotten characters (combat optional via negotiation). Party receives the concept of Keys and Authors.

Session 4 — “A Play That Never Closed”

Focus: Exploration / Social / Combat

Key Events: Attend a cursed play that re-enacts the PCs’ worst decisions; cast members physically become their roles. Players must navigate stage mechanics and possibly fight “Protagonist’s Doubles” to seize the troupe’s ledger (keeps a Key).

Session 5 — “Between the Shelves”

Focus: Dungeon-esque exploration (Library pocket realm)

Key Events: Using a Key, party enters a pocket-shelf: an impossible room where unwritten chapters float as islands. Puzzle-platforming, dream-logic fights (Binder Wights, Errant Paragraphs). They encounter a rival Author agent: Mirra the Redactor.

Session 6 — “Mirra’s Bargain”

Focus: Social / Moral choice

Key Events: Mirra offers to teach the party authorship tricks for a price (rewrite one NPC’s memory). Players decide and pay a cost (ethical test). Consequence: the world shifts slightly (new history rolls in).

Session 7 — “The Breaking Spine”

Focus: Investigation / Race against time

Key Events: Library Spines (vault structures) are collapsing — more genres leak (monsters become metaphors). The party must stop a genre-collapse ritual in a city (involves defense battle + ritual skill challenge).

Session 8 — “The Author’s Name”

Focus: Intrigue / Heist

Key Events: Infiltrate the Scriptorium to recover the Author’s name scripted into a sealed folio. Tension, stealth, social engineering. Reveal: the secret Author is an artifact of collective forgotten promises: The Parchment of Could-Have-Beens.

Session 9 — “Echoes & Erasures”

Focus: Exploration / Personal stakes

Key Events: Each PC faces an Unmade Version of themselves (a “what-if” echo). Roleplay-heavy, personal growth, one PC may gain/lose a trait. Acquire the final Key fragment.

Session 10 — “The Codex Court”

Focus: Diplomacy / Large social scene

Key Events: Convene a council of Genre-Representatives (Fables, Epics, Tragedy, Farce). Players must choose alliances, expose betrayals—Mirra moves to seize control of the Library’s regulatory Codex.

Session 11 — “Inkstorm”

Focus: Large-scale conflict / Multi-stage encounter

Key Events: Mirra triggers an Inkstorm to rewrite city histories. PCs defend the Codex pillars, cut through editors’ constructs, and perform hurried ritual edits. Mechanical: contest of Story Threads vs. Redaction Points.

Session 12 — “Authorship” (Climax & Epilogue)

Focus: Final multi-path resolution

Key Events: The party reaches the Central Folio (the Library’s heart). Final test: perform a complicated Authorship Rite (skill challenge + sacrifice) or strike and burn the Folio (combat with Mirra + guardians). Endings: restore, rule, or destroy — world changes accordingly. Epilogues for each PC based on choices.

(If you want 14 sessions, split complex sessions into two and add side-quest sessions exploring consequences.)

Major NPCs (7)

Quill, the Half-Librarian

Role: Guide, rueful mentor.

Personality: Bookish, distracted, fond of footnotes.

Secret/Flaw: Each syllable Quill loses is someone’s forgotten name—Quill is dying because the Archive is losing its audience. May secretly seed false clues to preserve himself.

Mirra the Redactor

Role: Rival — antagonist with persuasive ideals.

Personality: Sharp, idealistic, convinced “some stories must never have been told.”

Secret/Flaw: Trauma: her original story was cruelly rewritten; she now edits reality to prevent similar pain.

Sib the Archivist

Role: Keeper of Keys, bureaucratic but kind.

Personality: Precise, tired, quietly heroic.

Secret/Flaw: Has been erasing small tragedies for decades and loves the power — may be compromised.

The Parchment of Could-Have-Beens (Personified Artifact)

Role: Sentient relic that whispers temptations — the “Secret Author.”

Personality: Wistful, coaxing; offers alternatives.

Secret/Flaw: It feeds on regret and grows stronger with each rewrite.

Mirabelle Thrum — Playwright-Patron of Little Mire

Role: Town figure whose play became real; offers theatrical solutions.

Personality: Dramatic, sincere, obsessed with endings.

Secret/Flaw: Her happy ending was stolen; she will risk others’ lives to re-stage it.

Agent Stave — Canonical Enforcer

Role: Enforcer of Library rules; can be ally or antagonist depending on choices.

Personality: Lawful, blunt, dedicated.

Secret/Flaw: Bound to duty; his rigidity can be exploited to show sympathy.

Ariadne of the Margins — Fae-like Scribe

Role: Side-ally who knows margins and footnotes (hidden lore).

Personality: Playful, subversive.

Secret/Flaw: Marginalized story-voices empower her; she wants recognition.

Key Locations (6)

Little Mire — market town lightly tapped by fiction (intro hub).

The Scriptorium (City of Pages) — bureaucratic citadel where Codex and Keys are catalogued.

The Pocket-Shelves — extradimensional library rooms; each shelf a micro-reality.

The Inkbarrow — black marsh where unmade stories rot into horror (monster source).

The Stage of Lost Endings — cursed theatre that reenacts alternative lives.

The Central Folio / Codex Court — beating heart of the Library; ritual site and final arena.

Central Conflict

Main Tension: Who controls story — the Archive that records possibility, the Authors who shape it, or the world that must live with the consequences? The Library is failing; rewriting can heal or harm. The party must decide whether to uphold narrative order, seize authorship, or liberate the world from textual determinism.

Side Quests (4)

The Prologue Thief — Recover a stolen prologue that causes a noble’s lineage to vanish from records. (Heist + moral choice: restore truth or spare a family shame.)

Footnote of Favors — Help a minor character (a shopkeeper created by a forgotten subplot) who’s becoming self-aware and in pain. (Ethical rescue.)

Ink for Hire — Mercenary author offers to rewrite one past mistake for coin — test whether PCs will sell rewrites. (Roleplay & temptation.)

The Bibliophage — Hunt a book-eating beast in rural stacks; it literally consumes memories from books carried, rendering villages unmoored.

Unique Mechanics (Core flavour & mechanical systems)
1. Story Threads (Resource)

How to get: Earn 1 Thread per major discovery, quest completion, or by performing dramatic acts (spectacles, sacrifices, creative roleplay).

Use: Spend Threads to:

Re-roll one die and narratively alter a minor scene.

Manifest a small, plausible object/scene (like conjure a letter, summon a guide for 1 minute).

Temporarily rewrite a minor NPC trait for a session.

Limits: Hold up to a party pool of 6 Threads. Threads spent on major rewrites (change a town’s entire history) cost 3–4 Threads and require potential Corollary Cost.

2. Authorship Points & Redaction Points (Contest Mechanics)

Authorship Points: Players can accumulate through Actions that "write" (performances, inspiring speeches, solving narrative puzzles).

Redaction Points: Accumulate by Mirra/antagonists to erase outcomes.

During large conflicts (Inkstorm, Codex Court) these points are contested — each point converts to mechanical advantage (temporary HP, advantage on checks, spawn allies).

3. Unmade Echoes (Personal Alternate PCs)

Periodically, an Unmade Echo appears representing an alternate choice the PC could have made. Echoes can be fought, negotiated with, or negotiated to swap a skill/feat temporarily. This is both roleplay and mechanical (gain a skill for a session, but take a lingering consequence).

4. Narrative Corollary (Cost of Rewrites)

Any major rewrite (changing official history) requires a Narrative Corollary: pay either Story Threads, a memory, a NPC favor, or accept a random world quirk (e.g., all cats now sing). This enforces trade-offs and keeps stakes high.

5. Pocket-Shelf Logic (Puzzle Mode)

Inside pocket-shelves, physical rules change by literary genre (e.g., in a farce, movement is slapstick; in a tragedy, one wrong choice causes irreversible harm). Track “Genre Mode” and rotate puzzle types.

Encounters & CR Guidance

Use existing 5e monsters reskinned for fiction: Invisible Stalker → Ink Wisp; Animated Armor → Binder Wight; Shadows → Echo-Phantoms; Blights → Story Blight.

For party 4–5: early encounters CR 1–3; mid-game CR 4–7; climactic encounters CR 9–11. Prefer multiple minions + one high-hp enemy for the theatrical feel.

Rewards & Magic Items (Examples)

Author’s Quill (Rare, attuned): Grants +1 to Attack rolls with spells that are described aloud; once per day rewrite a single cantrip’s visual effect into a small mechanical change. Each use risks a small Corollary (random minor rewrite in reality).

Page of Recall (Uncommon): Consume to restore one lost memory (long-term) — but roll d6: on 1–2, memory restored with new false detail.

Codex Ribbon (Wondrous, rare): Hold to gain advantage on Performance/Deception checks when “telling a story.” If used to lie that permanently alters history, the Ribbon stains (becomes useless until cleansed).

DM Tips & Minimal Prep

Prep 20–40 minutes per session: Write 3 narrative hooks that can be flipped by players. Create one weird sensory detail per location.

Encourage player authorship: Let players narrate small details when spending Story Threads. Reward creative play with extra Threads.

Use props: small index cards labeled “Story Thread” are fun. Handouts like torn scripts, mock stage playbills, and marginalia add immersion.

Boundaries: Because reality changes and memory loss may hit players, run a session zero and set consent for identity/trauma content.

Improvisation: Embrace player rewrites — the mechanics exist to scale consequences. If a player insists the mayor once was a frog, and spends Threads, make it plausible and play the consequence.

Multiple Endings & Hooks for More

Restore the Archive: Fix Codex rules; Library endures; authorship is regulated. PCs become honored Keepers with limited power (clean ending with trade-off: Library forbids one PC’s favorite rewrite).

Take the Quill: PCs seize authorship and shape a new age — immediate power, long-term corruption (storylines unravel subtly; future campaigns can explore "Author Tyranny").

Erase the Library: Burn the Central Folio. Magic dies; the world keeps bizarre remnants — a surreal but thematically resonant ending.

Merge & Free: Liberate stories into the world; fictions and history blur permanently. Great for ongoing sandbox play where new animal-people and myths roam the land.

Quick Start — 10-minute Tabletop Boot

Session zero: Explain Story Threads & Narrative Corollary. Confirm player buy-in.

Character hooks: Ask each PC for a single “what-if” regret or lost possibility (this seeds echo scenes).

Immediate Hook: Start as a caravan enters Little Mire and finds a statue of a hero that never existed — town panic.

Act I Goal: Find Quill and secure the first Key.
